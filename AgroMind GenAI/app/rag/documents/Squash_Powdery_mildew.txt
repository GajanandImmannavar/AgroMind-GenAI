Disease Name: Powdery Mildew

Crop:
Squash

Scientific Name:
Podosphaera xanthii / Erysiphe cichoracearum

Disease Type:
Fungal Disease

Affected Plant Parts:
Leaves, stems, and sometimes fruits

Description:
Powdery Mildew is a common disease in squash plants. It spreads mainly in warm and dry weather. The disease forms a white powder-like layer on leaves and can weaken the plant if not controlled early.

Cause:
- Caused by fungi such as Podosphaera xanthii or Erysiphe cichoracearum
- Spreads in warm and dry weather
- Wind helps carry fungal spores from plant to plant

Favorable Conditions:
- Warm temperatures
- Dry weather with moderate humidity
- Poor air circulation
- Dense plant growth

Symptoms:
- White powder-like layer on leaves
- Leaves may turn yellow
- Leaves may dry and fall early
- Reduced plant growth in severe cases

Disease Spread:
- Spores spread through wind
- Infection spreads quickly between nearby plants
- Crowded plants increase disease spread

Steps to Make Plant Healthy:
1. Remove infected leaves from the plant.
2. Maintain proper spacing between plants.
3. Improve air circulation in the field.
4. Spray recommended fungicide early.
5. Monitor plants regularly.

Recommended Medicine:
- Sulfur fungicide
- Potassium bicarbonate spray
- Myclobutanil

How to Apply:
- Spray fungicide evenly on leaves.
- Apply when early symptoms appear.
- Repeat spraying every 7–10 days if necessary.

Cost per Acre (Approximate):
- Fungicide cost: ₹800 – ₹1700 per acre
- Labor cost: ₹300 – ₹700
- Total cost: about ₹1100 – ₹2400 per acre

Recovery Time:
Plants may improve in about 1–2 weeks if treated early.

Prevention:
- Maintain proper spacing between plants
- Improve air circulation
- Avoid excess nitrogen fertilizer
- Monitor crops regularly

Best Farming Practices:
- Regular field inspection
- Proper plant spacing
- Balanced fertilizer use
- Good weed control

Effect on Yield:
If not controlled, Powdery Mildew can reduce squash plant growth and fruit production.

Farmer Advice:
Check squash leaves regularly for white powder-like spots. Early fungicide spraying and good field management help control the disease.
